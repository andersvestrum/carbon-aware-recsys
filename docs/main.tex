\begin{abstract}

E-commerce recommender systems strongly influence which products users consider and purchase, yet sustainability signals such as Product Carbon Footprint (PCF) are rarely available at catalog scale. This paper studies carbon-aware product recommendation in a realistic setting where most items lack PCF labels. We first estimate product-level carbon footprints by training a predictive model on publicly available PCF datasets and product metadata, enabling carbon estimates to be generalized across large product catalogs. We then apply a carbon-aware post-hoc re-ranking strategy directly on top of relevance scores produced by established collaborative and sequential recommendation models, trading off predicted engagement and estimated product carbon footprint. We evaluate the proposed framework using the Amazon Reviews dataset as a large-scale proxy for user--item interactions. To capture variation across product domains, we focus on three representative categories: \textit{Home\_and\_Kitchen}, \textit{Sports\_and\_Outdoors}, and \textit{Electronics}. By varying the trade-off parameter $\lambda$, we construct Pareto frontiers that characterize the relationship between recommendation quality and environmental impact. Our results provide a reproducible framework for studying carbon-aware recommendation when PCF labels are sparse and must be inferred, enabling systematic analysis of engagement--carbon trade-offs, category-level differences, and limitations arising from observational interaction data such as exposure bias and missing-not-at-random feedback. \footnote{Code available at: \url{https://github.com/andersvestrum/carbon-aware-recsys}.}

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
 
Online retail has grown into one of the dominant channels through which consumers acquire
goods, and the environmental consequences of that growth have attracted sustained attention
across dimensions including shipping logistics, packaging waste, and return-driven reverse
supply chains~\cite{olah2023sustainable}. The scale of the problem is considerable: UN
Trade and Development estimates that global e-commerce sales reached USD~27 trillion in
2022~\cite{unctad2024ecommerce}, and the carbon consequences of that volume are
increasingly documented as a meaningful contributor to greenhouse gas
emissions~\cite{buldeorai2023ecommerce}. Importantly, the environmental footprint of
online retail is not uniform. It depends in substantial part on how purchasing decisions
are structured and presented to consumers~\cite{buldeorai2023ecommerce}.
 
At the policy level, this urgency has found expression in legislation. The European Union's
Directive 2024/825 on Empowering Consumers for the Green Transition mandates environmental
transparency at the point of consumer purchase and explicitly frames behavioral change as a
mechanism for achieving climate objectives~\cite{eu2024825}. More broadly, the European
Green Deal, whose policy documents exhibit strong alignment with SDG~12 on sustainable
consumption and production~\cite{koundouri2024greendeal}, treats demand-side interventions
as a structural component of decarbonization rather than a regulatory afterthought.
Recommender systems, as the primary interface through which consumers encounter products in
large online marketplaces, occupy precisely this intersection of algorithmic design and
consumption politics.
 
In large online marketplaces, recommender systems play a central role in determining which
products become visible to users, and can therefore indirectly shape patterns of
consumption~\cite{jesse2021digitalnudging, halimeh2026nudging}. By determining the set of
products a user is most likely to encounter, recommendation algorithms can potentially shift
demand toward lower-impact alternatives without restricting user choice.
 
Recent research has therefore begun to explore \emph{sustainability-aware recommender
systems}, where environmental signals such as carbon footprint are incorporated into
recommendation decisions~\cite{kalisvaart2025greenerrec, spillo2023sustainabilityaware,
ecoa2026, vicenti2026pcfllm}. One important sustainability signal is the \emph{Product
Carbon Footprint (PCF)}, typically measured in kilograms of CO$_2$ equivalent and estimated
using life-cycle assessment (LCA) methodologies~\cite{meinrenken2022carboncatalogue}.
Obtaining PCF values at scale is, however, genuinely difficult: LCA studies require
extensive supply-chain data and are rarely available for the long tail of products in large
e-commerce catalogs~\cite{meinrenken2022carboncatalogue}. As a result, most
recommender-system datasets lack item-level environmental impact information.
 
To address this limitation, recent work has proposed estimating PCF from product metadata
using large language models (LLMs) or other predictive approaches, enabling
sustainability-aware experimentation on standard recommendation
datasets~\cite{vicenti2026pcfllm, ecoa2026}. These studies demonstrate that environmental
signals can be incorporated into recommendation pipelines, often through post-hoc
re-ranking strategies that combine predicted item relevance with estimated environmental
impact.
 
In this work, we study carbon-aware recommendation in a realistic e-commerce setting where
PCF labels are missing for most items. Our approach follows a modular pipeline designed to
support strong baselines and reproducible evaluation. We first generate candidate
recommendations using established collaborative and sequential recommendation models
implemented in the RecBole framework~\cite{recbole2021}; these models produce relevance
scores that serve directly as the engagement signal in our re-ranking step. We then apply a
carbon-aware re-ranking strategy that trades off model-predicted relevance with estimated
product carbon footprint through a tunable parameter.
 
We evaluate this framework using the Amazon Reviews dataset as a large-scale proxy for
user--item interactions~\cite{amazon2023}, focusing on three representative product
categories: \textit{Home and Kitchen}, \textit{Sports and Outdoors}, and
\textit{Electronics}. Reviews serve as implicit signals of user engagement or purchase
behavior and are widely used in recommender-system research due to their scale and rich
product metadata~\cite{amazon2015, amazon2023, vicenti2026pcfllm, ecoa2026}. While such
observational data introduces well-known biases, including self-selection and exposure bias,
it enables controlled offline analysis of recommendation strategies at catalog scale.
 
Our goal is to analyze the trade-off between model-predicted relevance and the carbon
footprint of recommended products. By varying $\lambda$, we construct an engagement-carbon
Pareto frontier that characterizes how sustainability objectives interact with
recommendation performance across product categories and user segments.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
 
\subsection{Sustainability-Aware Recommender Systems}
 
Recent research has begun to incorporate environmental objectives into recommender systems.
Early work demonstrates that including carbon footprint information in recommendation
ranking can reveal trade-offs between recommendation accuracy and environmental
impact~\cite{spillo2023sustainabilityaware, ecoa2026, vicenti2026pcfllm}. Similarly,
sustainability-aware recommendation frameworks have shown that post-hoc re-ranking
strategies can reduce the average footprint of recommended items while preserving
acceptable levels of recommendation quality~\cite{kalisvaart2025greenerrec}. These studies
establish the feasibility of incorporating environmental signals into recommender pipelines,
but typically operate on domain-specific datasets with known footprint labels. Broader
surveys of the field have further identified a systematic gap in evaluation metrics that
account for sustainability outcomes such as reduced carbon footprint, and have positioned
recommender systems as instruments for achieving the UN Sustainable Development Goals
through behavioral influence~\cite{felfernig2023sustainability}.
 
\subsection{Estimating Product Carbon Footprints}

A central challenge for sustainability-aware recommendation is the scarcity of item-level
carbon footprint data. The Carbon Catalogue provides one of the few publicly available
datasets containing product-level PCF estimates derived from life-cycle
assessments~\cite{meinrenken2022carboncatalogue}. More recent work attempts to scale
footprint estimation using machine learning approaches, including LLM-based methods that
infer PCF values from product descriptions and metadata~\cite{vicenti2026pcfllm, ecoa2026}.

Parallel advances in in-context learning have shown that large language models can perform
structured prediction tasks with minimal task-specific supervision when provided with
representative labeled examples~\cite{dong2022icl, brown2020gpt3}. Retrieval-augmented
generation extends this by grounding model outputs in retrieved evidence rather than
parametric knowledge alone, substantially improving reliability on domain-specific numeric
tasks~\cite{gao2023ragsurvey}. Instructing models to reason step by
step before producing a final answer further improves numeric estimation
quality~\cite{wei2022cot}.

We build on these developments by employing a retrieval-augmented few-shot estimation
pipeline that combines semantic similarity search with structured LLM prompting to infer
PCF values for Amazon products from available metadata~\cite{amazon2015, amazon2023},
enabling large-scale experimentation with carbon-aware recommendation.
 
\subsection{Recommendation Infrastructure and Baseline Models}
 
Modern recommender-system research relies on standardized frameworks and strong baseline
models. RecBole provides a unified library implementing dozens of recommendation algorithms
with consistent evaluation protocols, facilitating reproducible experimentation across model
families~\cite{recbole2021}. The relevance scores produced by these models serve as direct
proxies for predicted user engagement in post-hoc re-ranking pipelines, making it
straightforward to incorporate additional objectives such as carbon footprint without
modifying the underlying recommendation model.
 
\subsection{Behavioral Effects of Recommendations and Digital Nudging}
 
Recommendation rankings influence user decisions by shaping the set of alternatives that users consider. Research in digital nudging has systematically documented this effect, demonstrating that recommender systems alter user behavior through mechanisms such as framing, salience, and ranking position~\cite{jesse2021digitalnudging}. The design of ranking criteria is therefore not a neutral technical decision; it constitutes a choice about which products receive attention and, by extension, which consumption patterns are reinforced.
In sustainability contexts, explanations and information framing have been shown to
significantly increase the likelihood that users select environmentally friendly
products~\cite{halimeh2026nudging}. Importantly, however, recommendation-driven behavioral
change is not always beneficial: simulation studies show that when systems are trained on
data already shaped by prior recommendations, a feedback loop emerges that homogenizes user
behavior without improving utility~\cite{chaney2018confounding}. This finding underscores
that the \emph{content} of what recommendation systems amplify matters, and provides
additional motivation for introducing carbon-aware objectives as a corrective to
engagement-only optimization. Reviews spanning nudging interventions from 2008 to 2024
confirm that digital nudging has emerged as a growing subfield, with ranking-based and
salience-based interventions among the most studied tools for promoting sustainable
choice~\cite{nudgereview2024}.
 
\subsection{Sustainability Evaluation Metrics}
 
Beyond traditional accuracy metrics, recent work proposes evaluation frameworks for
recommender systems that incorporate sustainability objectives. These include metrics based
on environmental impact, life-cycle assessment signals, and the proportion of recommended
items classified as environmentally friendly~\cite{felfernig2025sustainability}. Such work
highlights the importance of evaluating recommender systems along multiple objectives,
motivating the engagement-carbon trade-off analysis explored in this study.

\section{Background: Problem Context and Motivation}
\label{sec:background}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
 
The technical problem addressed in this paper, namely the integration of carbon signals
into recommender systems, sits at the intersection of three converging pressures: the
documented environmental footprint of e-commerce at scale, the political
institutionalization of sustainable consumption as a policy goal, and the growing
recognition that algorithmic systems represent a lever of behavioral change whose social
implications warrant deliberate design.
 
\paragraph{The environmental footprint of e-commerce.}
The environmental impact of online retail is substantial but heterogeneous. A systematic
review of over twenty carbon footprint assessments finds that e-commerce's environmental
performance relative to traditional retail varies considerably with consumer behavior,
delivery speed, return rates, and geographic context~\cite{buldeorai2023ecommerce}. This
heterogeneity is consequential: it implies that the composition of a consumer's purchase
basket, and in particular the products surfaced by recommendation algorithms, directly
shapes the carbon intensity of their shopping activity. Recommender systems are not
environmentally neutral; they function, in effect, as an unseen layer of consumption
infrastructure.
 
\paragraph{Policy context: the demand for algorithmic sustainability.}
Regulatory frameworks are beginning to reflect this reality. The European Green Deal,
launched in 2019 and operationalized through a series of legislative instruments,
explicitly targets sustainable consumption and production as a structural priority, with
strong alignment to SDG~12~\cite{koundouri2024greendeal}. The EU's Directive 2024/825 goes
further, mandating that consumers receive reliable environmental information at the point of
purchase and prohibiting unsubstantiated environmental claims~\cite{eu2024825}. While this
directive targets product labeling and retailer disclosure, its underlying logic, that
better-informed consumers make greener choices, applies with equal force to algorithmic
curation. A carbon-aware recommender system can be understood as a technical implementation
of the same transparency goal: rather than labeling products reactively, it proactively
elevates lower-carbon options within the consideration set.
 
\paragraph{Recommender systems as choice architecture.}
The behavioral mechanism that makes this intervention viable is well-established. A
recommender system is, by construction, a choice architect at scale: it determines which
products are salient, and salience is one of the most robust predictors of selection.
Deploying a carbon-aware re-ranking strategy is therefore not an arbitrary imposition of
external values on users; it is a deliberate choice about what the default choice
architecture should optimize for. The question is not whether to have a choice architecture,
since any ranking policy constitutes one, but rather which objectives that architecture
should serve.
 
This framing also bears on questions of user autonomy. As Bonicalzi et al.~\cite{bonicalzi2023autonomy} discuss, recommender systems present a genuine tension
between the efficiency gains of personalized curation and the risk of limiting users'
epistemic and decisional independence. Our framework navigates this tension by making the
trade-off parameter $\lambda$ explicit and tunable, rather than embedding a fixed
sustainability preference invisibly into the ranking. This transparency is consistent with
the accountability requirements emerging from the EU Digital Services Act~\cite{dsa2022},
which imposes transparency and user-control requirements for recommendation systems
operated by very large online platforms. Furthermore, research on algorithmic feedback loops
demonstrates that engagement-optimized systems can silently homogenize consumer behavior
over time~\cite{chaney2018confounding}, a dynamic that an explicit, auditable
sustainability objective is better positioned to counteract.
 
\paragraph{Existing technical approaches.}
Traditional collaborative filtering and neural models, including BPR, NeuMF, SASRec, and
LightGCN, have demonstrated strong performance in predicting user preferences from
historical interaction data~\cite{recbole2021}. These models, however, optimize for
engagement or relevance alone, without regard for the environmental impact of recommended
items. Recent advances in sustainability-aware recommendation have introduced environmental
signals into this pipeline, yet a fundamental challenge persists: PCF data is missing for
the majority of catalog items, limiting the direct application of sustainability-aware
objectives at scale \cite{meinrenken2022carboncatalogue}. Predictive modeling approaches
using product metadata and LLMs have been proposed to close this
gap~\cite{vicenti2026pcfllm, ecoa2026}. We build on these developments, combining
established recommendation baselines with a carbon-aware re-ranking strategy and evaluating
the resulting engagement-sustainability trade-offs in a reproducible, large-scale
experimental setting.



\section{Methodology}\label{sec:methodology}
Our approach consists of three components: (1) estimating product carbon footprints (PCF) for Amazon catalog items, (2) constructing a recommendation pipeline that combines candidate generation and carbon-aware re-ranking, and (3) evaluating the trade-off between recommendation quality and environmental impact.

\subsection{Product Carbon Footprint Estimation}

A central challenge in sustainability-aware recommendation is that PCF
labels are unavailable for most items in large e-commerce catalogs. To
address this, we estimate product-level carbon footprints by transferring
supervision from the Carbon Catalogue~\cite{meinrenken2022carboncatalogue}
to the Amazon catalog~\cite{amazon2023}. We compare three estimation
strategies of increasing sophistication on held-out Carbon Catalogue items
before applying the best-performing method downstream.

\paragraph{Nearest-neighbour average.}
As a non-parametric baseline, we embed each product title using the
all-MiniLM-L6-v2 sentence encoder~\cite{reimers2019sbert} and
retrieve the $k$ nearest labelled neighbours from the Carbon Catalogue
by cosine similarity. The PCF estimate is the unweighted average of the
neighbours' known PCF values. This baseline requires no language model
and serves as a strong non-parametric reference point.



\paragraph{Zero-shot LLM.}
As a second baseline, we prompt GPT-4.1 mini~\cite{openai2025gpt41mini}
with the product title alone, without any retrieved examples, and ask it
to estimate PCF in kg\,CO$_2$e. The prompt specifies
a typical PCF range (1--10{,}000 kg CO$_2$e) and a strict output format
(one number, no scientific notation); parsed values are clamped to a
plausible range before evaluation. This tests whether parametric world
knowledge encoded during pre-training is sufficient for numeric carbon
estimation, without any grounding in labeled product examples.

\paragraph{Few-shot retrieval-augmented LLM.}
Our primary estimator combines semantic retrieval with few-shot
prompting~\cite{brown2020gpt3}. For each query product,
we embed its title using \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}~\cite{reimers2019sbert}
and retrieve its five nearest Carbon Catalogue neighbours by cosine
similarity. These neighbours are assembled into a structured prompt in
which they serve as labelled in-context examples, as illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:llm-prompt}. The model is instructed to reason step by
step before producing a final PCF estimate~\cite{wei2022cot}, following
the retrieval-augmented estimation strategy of
Vicenti et al.~\cite{vicenti2026pcfllm}.

\paragraph{Evaluation and method selection.}
We evaluate all three methods on a held-out slice of 100 Carbon Catalogue
items, hiding the true PCF and scoring predictions by RMSE, MAE, and
Spearman rank correlation. The best-performing method is adopted as
the PCF estimator for all downstream experiments: for each Amazon catalog
item, we embed its title, retrieve the five nearest Carbon Catalogue
neighbours, and apply the few-shot prompt to obtain a product-level
estimate $\mathrm{PCF}_i$. Items with insufficient metadata for embedding
are excluded from downstream analysis.



\subsection{Recommendation Pipeline}

Our recommendation pipeline is modular and consists of three stages.

\paragraph{Data preparation.}
We process the Amazon Reviews dataset to construct user--item interaction histories. Each review is treated as implicit feedback indicating engagement. We split interactions into training, validation, and test sets by timestamp so that each user's test interactions occur strictly after their training interactions.

\paragraph{Candidate generation.}
For each user in the test set, we generate a candidate set of top-$K$ recommendations using four established algorithms implemented in the RecBole framework \cite{recbole2021}: BPR, NeuMF, SASRec, and LightGCN. Each model is trained on the training split and produces a relevance score $\hat{y}_{ui}$ for user $u$ and item $i$, which serves as our proxy for predicted engagement. Items are ranked by $\hat{y}_{ui}$, and the top-$K$ items are retained as candidate recommendations.

\paragraph{Carbon-aware re-ranking.}
We re-rank each candidate list by combining the model-predicted relevance score with the estimated product carbon footprint. For each candidate pair $(u,i)$, the final ranking score is

\begin{equation}
s_{ui} = (1 - \lambda)\, \hat{y}_{ui} - \lambda \, \mathrm{PCF}_i,
\end{equation}

where $\hat{y}_{ui}$ is the relevance score produced by the candidate generation model, $\mathrm{PCF}_i$ is the estimated product carbon footprint, and $\lambda \in [0,1]$ controls the trade-off between engagement and sustainability. When $\lambda = 0$, ranking depends only on model-predicted relevance; when $\lambda = 1$, ranking prioritizes lower-carbon items.

\subsection{Evaluation and Trade-off Analysis}

We evaluate the carbon-aware recommendation lists using both engagement and sustainability metrics.

\paragraph{Engagement metrics.}
We report NDCG@10 and Recall@10, which measure the quality of the top-10 recommended items using observed test interactions as ground truth.

\paragraph{Sustainability metric.}
We measure environmental impact using AvgPCF@10, defined as the average predicted product carbon footprint of items appearing in each user's top-10 recommendation list.

\paragraph{Carbon reduction.}
To provide an interpretable measure of environmental improvement, we additionally report the percentage reduction in recommended-item carbon footprint relative to the engagement-only baseline ($\lambda=0$):

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Reduction} =
\frac{\mathrm{AvgPCF}_{\lambda=0} - \mathrm{AvgPCF}_{\lambda}}
{\mathrm{AvgPCF}_{\lambda=0}}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Pareto frontier.}
To characterize the engagement--sustainability trade-off, we vary $\lambda$ over a fixed grid and compute NDCG@10, Recall@10, and AvgPCF@10 for each setting. We then plot NDCG@10 against AvgPCF@10 to obtain a Pareto frontier, identifying operating points for which no other configuration simultaneously achieves higher engagement and lower carbon footprint.




\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}
We evaluate the proposed framework on the Amazon Reviews dataset as a large-scale proxy for user--item interactions. The goal of our experiments is to quantify how much environmental impact can be reduced through carbon-aware re-ranking while preserving recommendation quality.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We follow the pipeline described in Section~4. For each product category, we train candidate generation models using the timestamp-based train/validation/test split and then apply carbon-aware re-ranking directly on the resulting relevance scores. Unless otherwise stated, all evaluation is performed on top-10 recommendation lists, and $\lambda$ is swept from $0$ to $1$ in fixed increments.

\subsection{Candidate Generation Baselines}

To test whether the effects of carbon-aware re-ranking are robust across recommendation paradigms, we use four standard RecBole models as candidate generators:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{BPR} (Bayesian Personalized Ranking), a pairwise collaborative filtering model for implicit feedback;
    \item \textbf{NeuMF} (Neural Matrix Factorization), a neural hybrid of matrix factorization and multi-layer perceptrons;
    \item \textbf{LightGCN} (Light Graph Convolutional Network), a graph-based collaborative filtering model defined over the user--item interaction graph.
\end{itemize}

These baselines span collaborative, neural, and graph-based recommendation families.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

For each candidate generation baseline, we apply carbon-aware re-ranking over a sweep of $\lambda$ values and evaluate the resulting top-10 recommendation lists using NDCG@10, Recall@10, AvgPCF@10, and carbon reduction relative to the $\lambda=0$ baseline. This protocol allows us to measure both the recommendation cost and the environmental benefit of increasing the weight placed on carbon footprint.

\subsection{Research Questions}

Our experiments are designed to answer the following questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} How does carbon-aware re-ranking affect recommendation quality across different candidate generation models?
    \item \textbf{RQ2:} How much reduction in recommended-item carbon footprint can be achieved while maintaining acceptable engagement performance?
    \item \textbf{RQ3:} Do different recommendation algorithms exhibit different engagement--sustainability trade-offs?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Trade-off Visualization}

To analyze the effect of carbon-aware re-ranking, we plot NDCG@10 against AvgPCF@10 for each value of $\lambda$ and for each candidate generation model. These curves define Pareto frontiers that summarize the achievable trade-off between engagement and environmental impact. We additionally report carbon reduction percentages to quantify the practical benefit of moving away from the engagement-only baseline.

Overall, this experimental design enables a systematic comparison of carbon-aware re-ranking across multiple recommendation backbones and provides a reproducible framework for studying sustainability objectives in large-scale e-commerce recommendation.


\section{Prelimenary Results}\label{sec:results}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{PCF Estimation Quality}
\label{sec:pcf-results}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Before evaluating the downstream recommendation trade-off, we first assess
the quality of the PCF estimation module itself. The environmental signal
used during re-ranking is only as reliable as the estimator used to assign
PCF values to catalog items.

\paragraph{Evaluation setup.}
We evaluate the three estimation methods on a held-out slice of 100 Carbon
Catalogue products. For each labeled item, we hide the true PCF, retrieve
the top-5 nearest labeled neighbours from the remaining catalogue, and
generate predictions using: (1) a nearest-neighbour average baseline,
(2) a zero-shot LLM baseline using GPT-4.1 mini~\cite{openai2025gpt41mini},
and (3) a retrieval-augmented few-shot LLM baseline. We report root mean
squared error (RMSE), mean absolute error (MAE), and Spearman rank correlation.

The retrieval-augmented few-shot method achieves the best Spearman rank correlation
(0.853), indicating that retrieved examples provide useful calibration and better preserve
the relative ordering of products by carbon footprint. However, the nearest-neighbor
baseline achieves both the lowest RMSE (3{,}964) and the lowest MAE (1{,}326), making it
the most accurate method in absolute error terms on this held-out slice. The few-shot
method has higher RMSE (8{,}328) and MAE (1{,}696), likely due to a small number of larger
errors that are amplified by RMSE. The zero-shot baseline is the weakest
(lowest Spearman 0.421, highest MAE 3{,}334). We mitigate scale and unit
confusion by stating a typical PCF range and output format in the prompt
and clamping parsed values; without these measures, zero-shot exhibited
catastrophic scale errors (RMSE/MAE in the tens or hundreds of millions).
With the fixes in place, zero-shot predictions lie in a plausible range
but still lag both baselines.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
These results support retrieval-augmented few-shot prompting as the
preferred PCF estimation strategy. The nearest-neighbor baseline remains a
strong non-parametric reference and wins on RMSE. Zero-shot remains
insufficient for stable numeric PCF estimation without in-context examples,
consistent with prior work on domain-specific numeric
calibration~\cite{dong2022icl, hegselmann2023tabllm}.

\paragraph{Scope and limitation.}
These results should be interpreted as preliminary rather than final. The
evaluation is based on a fixed 100-example slice used to control API cost
for the LLM-based methods, which is sufficient to establish an early signal
about method quality but not to support strong claims about generalization.
The reported numbers are from a run with scale/format prompt and output
clamping applied to zero-shot and few-shot. In the final version, this
analysis will be extended either to the full Carbon Catalogue evaluation
set or to repeated fixed-seed subsamples with confidence intervals.

\subsection{Carbon-Aware Recommendation: Engagement--Carbon Trade-off}
\label{sec:rec-results}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

We report preliminary recommendation results for the Electronics category
across three candidate generation models: BPR, NeuMF, and LightGCN.
Results for the remaining categories (Home and Kitchen, Sports and
Outdoors) are pending and will be included in the final
version.

\paragraph{Overall pattern.}
Across all three models, carbon-aware re-ranking consistently reduces the
average carbon footprint of recommended items as $\lambda$ increases, at
the cost of engagement quality. Critically, the engagement cost remains
near zero for a wide range of intermediate $\lambda$ values before
dropping sharply at high carbon weights ($\lambda \geq 0.95$). This
plateau structure suggests that substantial carbon reductions are
achievable with minimal sacrifice to recommendation quality, addressing
RQ1 and RQ2.

\paragraph{BPR.}
BPR achieves the strongest engagement performance in Electronics, with a
baseline NDCG@10 of 0.622 at $\lambda = 0$. The $\lambda$ sensitivity
plot shows that NDCG@10 remains effectively flat across the full range
$\lambda \in [0, 0.90]$, while average carbon footprint decreases
monotonically from approximately 2{,}100 to 1{,}300\,kg CO$_2$e. The
Pareto frontier confirms that operating points at $\lambda \in [0.80,
0.90]$ achieve roughly 10--35\% carbon reduction while preserving
NDCG@10 above 0.620. Only at $\lambda \geq 0.95$ does engagement begin
to degrade meaningfully, falling to 0.577 at $\lambda = 1.00$.

\paragraph{LightGCN.}
LightGCN achieves a baseline NDCG@10 of 0.393, roughly 37\% lower than
BPR. Despite weaker absolute engagement, it exhibits a similarly
favourable trade-off structure: NDCG@10 remains stable across $\lambda
\in [0, 0.85]$ while carbon decreases steadily from approximately
1{,}900 to 700\,kg CO$_2$e. The sharp engagement cliff occurs later
than for NeuMF, making LightGCN a more robust backbone for
sustainability-aware re-ranking than NeuMF in this category.

\paragraph{NeuMF.}
NeuMF performs substantially worse in Electronics, with a baseline
NDCG@10 of only 0.070. The $\lambda$ sensitivity plot reveals an
earlier and steeper engagement cliff: NDCG@10 begins declining already
at $\lambda \approx 0.75$, and carbon reduction is near-complete by
$\lambda = 0.50$ (dropping from approximately 1{,}500 to
300\,kg CO$_2$e). The Pareto frontier shows that NeuMF achieves
meaningful carbon reductions but at proportionally higher engagement
cost, indicating that models with weaker baseline relevance scores are
more sensitive to carbon re-weighting.

\paragraph{Cross-model comparison.}
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto-comparison} compares the Pareto frontiers of all

achieving both higher engagement and comparable carbon reductions

NDCG@10 = 0.622 at approximately 1{,}320\,kg CO$_2$e average carbon,

\section{Limitations and Future Work}\label{sec:limitations}
While our framework enables systematic analysis of engagement–sustainability trade-offs in recommender systems, several limitations should be acknowledged.

\paragraph{Observational data biases.}
Our experiments rely on the Amazon Reviews dataset as a proxy for user--item interactions. While widely used in recommender-system research, review datasets introduce several well-known biases. First, only a small fraction of purchases result in reviews, leading to \emph{selection bias} in which reviewers may not represent the broader population of users. Second, the absence of a review does not necessarily indicate negative feedback, creating challenges due to \emph{missing negatives}. Finally, review data may be affected by \emph{temporal confounds}, including changes in product popularity, seasonality, and shifting consumer trends. These limitations are inherent to many offline recommender-system benchmarks and may affect the interpretation of engagement metrics.

\paragraph{Carbon footprint estimation uncertainty.}
Our approach relies on predicted product carbon footprints derived from metadata and external PCF datasets. Although predictive models enable PCF estimation at catalog scale, these estimates may contain significant uncertainty compared to full life-cycle assessments. Errors in PCF estimation could propagate into the re-ranking stage, potentially affecting the measured engagement--sustainability trade-offs.

\paragraph{Offline evaluation limitations.}
The experiments are conducted using offline recommendation evaluation, which cannot fully capture how users would respond to sustainability-aware recommendations in a real-world setting. In practice, user behavior may change when exposed to environmentally informative signals, explanations, or interface changes. Online A/B testing or controlled behavioral experiments would provide stronger evidence of the real-world impact of carbon-aware recommendation strategies.

\paragraph{Computational sustainability trade-offs.}
An additional consideration is the environmental footprint of the machine learning systems themselves. While the computational cost of models used for PCF estimation and candidate generation is typically small compared to the life-cycle emissions of physical products, large-scale recommendation systems may still incur non-negligible energy consumption. Understanding the relationship between the environmental benefits of carbon-aware recommendations and the computational footprint of the underlying models remains an important direction for future work.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Several directions could extend the present study. First, future work could evaluate carbon-aware recommendation using richer behavioral datasets that contain full browsing and purchase histories rather than relying on review-based proxies. Second, improving PCF estimation methods, for example through structured supply chain data or domain-specific carbon models, could reduce uncertainty in sustainability signals. Third, integrating carbon-aware objectives directly into recommendation model training instead of applying post-hoc re-ranking may yield more efficient multi-objective recommendation strategies. Finally, online experiments and user studies could investigate how sustainability-aware recommendations influence real purchasing decisions and whether transparency or explanation mechanisms increase user acceptance of environmentally informed rankings.

\subsection{Retrieval-Based Product Carbon Footprint Estimation}
\label{sec:pcf-retrieval}

A central challenge in sustainability-aware recommendation is that product-level carbon
footprint labels are unavailable for the vast majority of items in large e-commerce
catalogs. Rather than assuming direct access to item-level life-cycle assessments, we
estimate Product Carbon Footprint (PCF) values for Amazon products by transferring
information from a smaller labeled reference set, the Carbon Catalogue
\cite{meinrenken2022carboncatalogue}, to a much larger unlabeled retail catalog.

\paragraph{Semantic product representation.}
We begin with two data sources: the Carbon Catalogue, which provides product titles and
known PCF values, and Amazon product metadata, which provides item titles and additional
attributes such as category, brand, and description. For each product, we construct a text
representation from the available metadata and encode it using a sentence embedding model.
This produces a shared semantic space in which products with similar titles and descriptions
are mapped to nearby vectors even when their wording differs.

\paragraph{Nearest-neighbor retrieval.}
For each Amazon product, we compute cosine similarity between its embedding and the
embeddings of labeled Carbon Catalogue products, then retrieve the top-$5$ most similar
neighbors. These retrieved products serve as an interpretable bridge between the labeled
and unlabeled domains: each estimated PCF value can be traced back to a small set of
semantically related products with known footprints.

\paragraph{PCF estimation strategies.}
We evaluate three estimation strategies built on top of the retrieved neighbors. The first
is a non-parametric nearest-neighbor baseline that predicts PCF as the average footprint of
the top-$5$ retrieved products. The second is a zero-shot LLM baseline that predicts PCF
from the query product text alone. The third is a retrieval-augmented few-shot LLM method,
in which the retrieved neighbors and their known PCF values are inserted into the prompt as
in-context examples before predicting the footprint of the target product. This allows us
to test whether semantic retrieval alone is sufficient, and whether explicitly exposing the
retrieved examples improves estimation relative to zero-shot prompting.

\paragraph{Few-shot prompting.}
For the few-shot method, the prompt contains the titles and known PCF values of the
retrieved Carbon Catalogue neighbors followed by the target Amazon product. The model is
then asked to return a single numeric estimate in kilograms of CO$_2$ equivalent. This
setup preserves interpretability while allowing the language model to interpolate from
nearby labeled examples rather than estimating from product text in isolation.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.}
To evaluate the quality of the PCF estimator, we use the Carbon Catalogue itself as a
held-out benchmark. For each labeled product, we temporarily hide its true PCF value,
retrieve its nearest neighbors from the remaining Carbon Catalogue items, generate a
prediction with each method, and compare the prediction to the ground truth. We report root
mean squared error (RMSE), mean absolute error (MAE), and Spearman rank correlation. This
evaluation setup measures both absolute estimation error and the degree to which the method
preserves the relative ordering of products by carbon intensity.

\paragraph{Use in the recommendation pipeline.}
After selecting a PCF estimation method, we apply it to the Amazon catalog to assign an
estimated footprint $\mathrm{PCF}_i$ to each product. These estimated PCF values are then
used as the environmental signal in the downstream carbon-aware re-ranking stage.

\subsection{Preliminary Results: PCF Estimation Quality}
\label{sec:pcf-results-draft}

Before evaluating the downstream recommendation trade-off, we first assess the quality of
the product carbon footprint (PCF) estimation module itself. This step is important because
the environmental signal used during re-ranking is only as reliable as the estimator used
to assign PCF values to catalog items.

\paragraph{Evaluation setup.}
We evaluate the retrieval-based PCF estimator on held-out Carbon Catalogue products. For

retrieval-augmented few-shot LLM baseline. We report root
